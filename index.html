Evaporation

The heat of the sun causes water from the surface of water bodies such as oceans, streams, and lakes to evaporate into water vapour in the atmosphere.

Plants also release water vapor through their leaves, while animals contribute through respiration and sweating. This biological water release is called transpiration.

Condensation

As water vapour rises higher into the atmosphere, it encounters cooler temperatures where it condenses back into liquid water onto tiny particles of dust or salt in the air.

When trillions of these droplets cluster together densly enough, they form visible clouds.

Precipitation

Once water droplets clumping together in clouds become heavy enough, gravity takes over and they fall as precipitation. Depending on temperature and atmospheric conditions, precipitation occurs as rain, snow, sleet, or hail.

Collection

Water returns to the ground and collects in various reservoirs: oceans, lakes, glaciers, and underground aquifers. Some remains in these storage areas for long periods, while some quickly returns to the cycle through evaporation.

Infiltration Some water seeps into the ground through soil and rock layers. This infiltrated water replenishes underground aquifers and groundwater systems, where it can remain stored for long periods or leak into rivers and lakes.
Runoff Some water flows across the land's surface through streams and rivers toward larger water bodies. This runoff shapes landscapes and carries minerals and nutrients along its path.
